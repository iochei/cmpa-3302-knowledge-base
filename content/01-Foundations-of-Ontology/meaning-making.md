Information Architecture is fundamentally about semantics—the study of meaning. As architects, we don't just organize content; we organize meaning. The goal is to ensure that the meaning intended by the publisher matches the meaning interpreted by the user.

### The Triangle of Meaning

A useful model for understanding semantics in IA is the Ogden-Richards "Triangle of Meaning," which illustrates the relationship between a thought, a symbol (word/label), and a referent (the actual object or concept).

*   **Symbol:** The label we use (e.g., "Dog").
*   **Thought (Reference):** The concept the user has in their mind when they see the symbol (e.g., the mental image of a golden retriever).
*   **Referent:** The actual, real-world object (e.g., the physical dog in front of you).

### The IA Challenge

The critical link in this triangle is between the **Symbol** (your label) and the **Thought** (the user's interpretation).

If you label a section "Blog" (Symbol), but your user interprets "Blog" as "Opinion Piece" while another interprets it as "Company News" (different Thoughts), your semantic IA has failed. We use user research (like card sorting) to align our symbols with users' thoughts.
