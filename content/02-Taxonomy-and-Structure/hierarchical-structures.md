Hierarchies are the most common way we organize information. They create a parent-child relationship between content, forming a tree structure. In Information Architecture, this is often visualized and implemented as a sitemap.

### Approaches to Hierarchy

Information Architects use two primary approaches to define these structures:

#### 1. Top-Down Approach
*   **Definition:** Starting from the broad and moving to the specific.
*   **Process:** You define the main categories (e.g., "Products", "About", "Support") first, based on business goals and high-level user needs. Then, you break those categories down into sub-pages.
*   **Best For:** New websites where you are defining the overall structure from scratch. It establishes a strong "information scent" from the homepage.

#### 2. Bottom-Up Approach
*   **Definition:** Starting from the specific content and grouping it into broader categories.
*   **Process:** You audit all existing content assets (documents, articles, data), identify relationships between them, and then create categories to group them logically.
*   **Best For:** Large, existing content repositories or migrations where you need to make sense of a disorganized mass of information.

### The Balance

Most successful IAs are a mix of both. You use a **top-down** process to design the primary navigation, but you rely on **bottom-up** analysis of your content metadata to ensure the detailed pages are correctly tagged and cross-linked.
