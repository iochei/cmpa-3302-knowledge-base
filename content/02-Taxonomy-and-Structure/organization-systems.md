Organization systems form the backbone of any information architecture. They define how different pieces of content are grouped, related, and presented. In IA, we generally categorize these systems into two main types: Exact and Ambiguous.

### 1. Exact Organization Systems

These systems divide information into well-defined, mutually exclusive sections. Retrieval is straightforward and predictable because the user generally knows exactly what they are looking for.

*   **Alphabetical:** A to Z listing (e.g., an index or dictionary).
*   **Chronological:** By date (e.g., a blog archive, a historical timeline).
*   **Geographical:** By physical location (e.g., a store directory by floor, a map).

### 2. Ambiguous Organization Systems

These systems divide information into categories that are subjective, overlapping, or unclear. Retrieval requires browsing and exploration because the user may not know the exact terminology used.

*   **Topic/Subject:** By topic (e.g., "News", "Sports", "Technology" sections on a website).
*   **Task:** By what the user wants to do (e.g., "Apply for a Loan", "Report an Outage" on a government site).
*   **Audience:** By user group (e.g., "For Students", "For Faculty", "For Alumni" on a university site).

### The Hybrid Approach

Most effective websites use a **hybrid organization system**. They might use an ambiguous system (Topic) for top-level navigation and an exact system (Alphabetical) for a specific index within a category.
