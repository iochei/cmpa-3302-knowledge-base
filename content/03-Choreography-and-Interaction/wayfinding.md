Wayfinding is a term borrowed from architecture and urban planning that refers to the systems used by people to orient themselves and navigate from one location to another. In Information Architecture, we apply these same principles to digital spaces to ensure users always know:

1.  **Where am I?**
2.  **Where have I been?**
3.  **Where can I go next?**

### Key Tools for IA Wayfinding

Information Architects employ several standard UI patterns to support wayfinding:

*   **Breadcrumbs:** A horizontal trail of links (e.g., `Home > Category > Sub-page`) showing the user's hierarchical path. (See [Hierarchical Structures](02-Taxonomy-and-Structure/hierarchical-structures.md)).
*   **Persistent Global Navigation:** A menu that appears on every page, anchoring the user to the main sections of the site. (See [Navigational Schemas](02-Taxonomy-and-Structure/navigational-schemas.md)).
*   **Site Maps:** A high-level overview of the entire site's structure.
*   **Search Bars:** A primary tool for users who don't want to navigate the hierarchy. (See [Search System Interfaces](03-Choreography-and-Interaction/search-interfaces.md)).
*   **Clear Page Titles and Headings:** Visually grounding the user when they land on a new page.

Wayfinding is the user's experience of your [Navigational Schemas](02-Taxonomy-and-Structure/navigational-schemas.md). When wayfinding fails, users get "lost" in the information space.
