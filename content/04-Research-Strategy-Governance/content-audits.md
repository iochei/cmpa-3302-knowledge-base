Before you can restructure an information environment (especially in a redesign project), you must know exactly what you have. This is the purpose of content audits and inventories. While often used interchangeably, they serve distinct purposes in the IA process.

### 1. Content Inventory
*   **Definition:** A quantitative catalog of all content assets in the system.
*   **What it records:** A simple list. Every URL, page title, document, image, or database entry. It answers the question: "What is here?"
*   **How it's done:** Often automated using site-crawling tools. The result is a massive spreadsheet.

### 2. Content Audit
*   **Definition:** A qualitative review and analysis of the content assets.
*   **What it records:** It goes beyond the inventory to assess the content's quality, relevance, accuracy, and effectiveness. It answers the questions: "Is this content any good? Is it up-to-date? Does it support user needs?"
*   **How it's done:** A manual process performed by content strategists and information architects. Content might be graded (e.g., Keep, Revise, Delete).

### IA's Reliance on Audits

The content audit provides the foundational data for the **bottom-up** [Taxonomy](02-Taxonomy-and-Structure/hierarchical-structures.md#2-bottom-up-approach) process. You cannot define new categories or [Faceted Classification](02-Taxonomy-and-Structure/faceted-classification.md) schemas without first understanding the attributes and subjects of the existing content.
