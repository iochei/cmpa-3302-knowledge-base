To build an effective Information Architecture, you must understand how your users think, speak, and search. We don't guess the IA; we use empirical user research methods to inform our decisions.

### Key IA Research Methods

1.  **Card Sorting:**
    *   **Purpose:** To understand how users categorize information and what terminology they use.
    *   **Method:** Users are given cards with content topics (representing your intended [Ontology](01-Foundations-of-Ontology/what-is-ia.md#key-components-of-ia)) and asked to group them and label the groups themselves. This helps define your [Taxonomy](02-Taxonomy-and-Structure/hierarchical-structures.md).
2.  **Tree Testing (Reverse Card Sorting):**
    *   **Purpose:** To validate the proposed site hierarchy.
    *   **Method:** Users are given a text-only version of your site structure (the "tree") and asked to find specific items. It tests the structure without the influence of visual design. This validates your [Navigational Schemas](02-Taxonomy-and-Structure/navigational-schemas.md).
3.  **Content Audits and Inventories:** (See next page).
    *   **Purpose:** To analyze existing assets.
4.  **Usability Testing:**
    *   **Purpose:** To observe users interacting with the live (or prototype) IA to identify friction points in [Wayfinding](03-Choreography-and-Interaction/wayfinding.md).

### Research-Driven IA

IA is a user-centered discipline. Research ensures that the resulting structure matches the user's mental model of the information space, rather than the internal structure of the organization.
