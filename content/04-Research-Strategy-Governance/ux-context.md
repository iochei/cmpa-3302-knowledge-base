Information Architecture does not exist in a vacuum; it is a critical subset of User Experience (UX) Design. While UX encompasses the entire scope of a user's interaction with a product, IA focuses specifically on the organization, structure, and labeling of the information within that product.

### IA's Role in the UX Honeycomb

Peter Morville's "UX Honeycomb" is a useful framework for understanding this relationship. For a user experience to be meaningful, information must be:

1.  **Useful:** Does it fulfill a user's need? (IA organizes useful content).
2.  **Usable:** Is it easy to use? (IA structures it for easy navigation).
3.  **Desirable:** Is the design engaging? (IA supports this through clear [Labeling](03-Choreography-and-Interaction/labeling-systems.md)).
4.  **Findable:** Can users locate the content? (This is the primary goal of IA. See [Wayfinding](03-Choreography-and-Interaction/wayfinding.md)).
5.  **Accessible:** Can people with disabilities use it? (IA supports accessibility through clear structure and semantic HTML).
6.  **Credible:** Is the information trustworthy? (IA contributes to credibility through professional organization and clear sourcing).
7.  **Valuable:** Does it deliver value to the user and the organization?

### The UX Goal

A successful UX integrates these facets. An unusable, unfishable site (bad IA) cannot provide a good user experience, no matter how visually pleasing the design.
